Nov 16, 1942

**Trip to Phila**

Round trip cost $0.56. The whole distance about every mile you come to a suburb station. Those towns are about the age of Swarthmore approx 1-3 yrs, I guess. Sometimes in between you see a few farms. But west of the way you see residential homes. In the towns, some have factories. You certainly realize that this is the industrial sector of the country with all those factories, coal silos, and many have armed guards by the farms. What I saw of Phila was just like Chicago - lots of dirty buildings. I really appreciate the fact that Swarthmore is in the country. It's just like the Capitol Park in Sacto. Back to Phila, some of the streets are cobbled (bricks) and narrow (distance between buildings across the streets about same as between the road between blocks in Tule). In these streets they have street cars running and heavy traffic sometimes and facing these narrow streets uou see tall skyscrapers. Don't get me wrong - lots of big wide streets too.

Another thing I noticed was that they use a number of houses for draying things like furniture, vegetables, ice, etc. I noticed it in Chicago too and remarked about it to Sumis but he said you see lots of them. Another thing: the suburban railroads are elevated in the city and most of the stations are underground really big with escalators. At this meeting, it seems that the Friends' Mission Board has a Japanese-American Fellowship Committee which sponsors about twice a year these gatherings where all the Japanese in and around Philadelphia are invited. It seems that the Chairman of this committee Mr. Y. Passmoore Elkinton has travelled quite intensively in Japan and liked it. I think he said that his aunt was the wife of Dr. Nitole who represented Japan in the League of Nations.

Another man Mr. Walter Borden also was there usually takes the whole bunch to his house someplace every year. This year because of the war he couldn't do it. Shucks!

People I met: Rath Dohi is a sophomore here. She's from near San Luis Obajo (or something) and used to go to V.C. Walter Kato and Gus Tanako both from the northwest are at Haverford. Kenny Mussel went to Haverford first but couldn't find the courses he wanted so he transferred to Temple U. Bernice Abe is from Hawaii and she said she used to go to S.J.C. but she came east before evacuation. Another gal Katherine Takushita I think that's her name, is working in a home in Swarthmore. I hardly ever see any of them on the campus. Gus and Walter are coming over next Saturday for the football game between Swarthmore and Haverford. A funny thing: Mr. Elkinton is a Haverford grad and Mrs. Elkinton is a Swarthmorean, and two colleges are the biggest rivals. Some of the other people there were students, doctors, dentists, YMCA men, businessmen (I can't remember their names).

Last Nite at Dean Hunts' he invited 5 of us over. One fellow, Lee Borah, senator Borah's son was there too. It seems that Dean Hunt, his wife and Senator Borah were schoolmates. We just sorta talked about everything and nothing in particular. He served crackers, donuts, and a concoction of grape juice, orange juice, cloves, lemon and a few other things - warmed. It tasted kinda queer, but heck, I been tasting a lotta queer things since I cam here which I don't know what I'm eating. Last Monday noon they served some meatballs made from some leftovers. It had a funny taste and sorta smelled like cheese. I took it down with the rest of the fellows at the table. That's right! It was rotten. All last week my stomach felt like a overloaded gas tank. It's O.K. now.

Saturday night some fellow came here drunk about 2 PM and turned on the fire hose in the hall in the dorm. The halls were just like canals and some fellows' rooms were a couple inches deep in H2O and they leaked through the floor to the floor below and drenched some poor fellow's bed and closet.

It's really cold here too. I really appreciate that overcoat now. My room in Wharton is steam-heated by a radiator.

One thing that hit me right square in the face at that meeting in Phila in that the longer the people are in camp, the worse fitted they are to take their place in normal American society. If this invitation is to return to Japan on her possessions it's a different story (or is it?). In fact I think to return to any society and be useful city is going to require a big change in the paycheck of the service. That's one of the reasons why I'm hoping that Willie can get out to school. Really - I spend lots of time at night thinking what's going to happen to us after this war's over. I also often wonder what God's purpose was in selecting me to come where I never expected to, and when I never expected to. You can be some that I'll do my best to prepare for whatever took he intends to use me for.

There's a lot of things to write about but so much for now. Midterms are here.

George

P.S. I'm including a few leaves to prove that winter is coming. Most of the trees are bare now except the evergreens.
