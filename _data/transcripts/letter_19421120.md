Nov. 20, 1942

Hi!!

Last night I went to hear the Budapest String Quartet. It was really something. They played quartets composed by Hayden, Shostakovitch, and Beethoven. Their tone, timing, and everything was perfect. The cellist especially stood out (I noticed him most) because he moved his body and head like they do in those Walt Disney films and I do mean shake! However he really gets the music out. Tonight they had to annual "Hamburg Show" which is just like those varsity shows they used to give back at S.H.S. and S.J.C. Composed tho the talent at Tule Lake, personally, I think you got the better end. After all when you get 15,000 people and pick the cream you really got something. By the way, it cost $0.35, but the amount was free because it was sponsored by the William J. Cooper Foundation which is Swarthmore's own school catalog. After the show, we had a pep rally for the Haverford game tomorrow. We had a big campfire out on the football field. Oh yeah and the band played the same tune the one in Tule Lake played - you know - sol mi-noldo-mine do ti do fa! They played it just as rotten. I think we have better Gator-Taribers back in Tule too. Oh well, we can't have everything you know.

Funny thing - you know how we used to holler for soap about the time we reached Tule. Well a number of fellas spoke to me and said "I understand they can't get soap in those concentration camps." It seems that if you holler long enough and work enough it gets around the whole country. When I tell them that 75% (about) are American citizens, they're really surprised. One of my jobs out here is to set them right on some of the notions they have about us.

Have you heard from Otani-san or Mrs. Ogana yet? I'd like to write to them. Boy! I really feel sorry for those kids (Toshi, Konbo, and Takabo). I been reading a book by Alfred Adler - "What Life Should Mean to You." Mr. Adler, a psychologist claims that the whole life of an individual is determined during his first five years. That is, the attitudes and interpretation of incidents during one's lifetime. Imagine having to spend those important years in a concentration camp and away from their father. Boy, I hope this war is over soon. For their sake and the rest of the world too.

They have a radio broadcasting station on the campus not much power. I was thinking of getting in on it. Its not short-wave something to worry about there. A couple nights ago they left the transmitter on all night and found out something so they're going to rebuild it. They don't seem to have many people who know much about the technical end of it so I might be able to help. They have a FM receiver in the studio too. Boy! It really sounds real. There's about 5 FM stations in Phila. which play music mostly. New York stations are too far. It seems that our station doesn't have any priorities so I can't have them buy tubes for me. I guess I'll either have to have Walter Kale at Haverford have their station get them for me (they have an A1) or else wait till we get priorities cause we'll probably need them anyway to get parts to fix the transmitter. How's your radio working? I received the pop mechanics shop notes yesterday. Thanks. How about some more Tulcan Dupatches? I suggest that Miyo go to the library once in a while and read if you're not doing so already. Don't be a book worm though. I'm glad to know that Willie's sleeping at home. Its too far to go after you leave (slightly). Tell pop not to work too hard - just enough to get hungry - no more. Tell mom not to over-exert herself. Well, children, good night, sleep tight, don't let the fears bite. Signing off at 12:10 AM.

George
