Nov. 29, 1942

Hi — I didn't see [the] Dean Hunt yet because he was busy yesterday, but his secretary told me that some arrangements were being made for a place for me to stay during the vacation.

Last night (Sat.) there were movies in Clothier. They were a Walt Disney short on the fable of the grass-hopper & the ant (grasshopper played in summer while the ants worked etc), a travelogue on the Mediteranean Sea & the bordering countries & cities, an exploration thru an Alaskan Mountain range, and the main feature which was a story based on the lives of the natives of Green land. The picture was actually taken there, and is more of a description of the lives these estimos lead than anything else. After seeing the faces of these estimos, I'm convinced that they are of oriental origin. Their form of existance is very primitive - they live in seal-skin tents on the rocky & snowy land & subsist on fish, berries & occasional animal meat. It's a wonder to me that they didn't migrate south to a more confortable place. The picture ended with a statement going some what as follows: "And so we see that these people can & do live happily in a very primitive civilzation. They have their loves, fears, friendships and enemies and festivals. To them their existence is a paradise."

All this may be true, but to us who have known civilzation & who know or see its potentialities, it would be impossible to live such a life. It is our destiny that we should always strive for better things, a better world, and finally, to achieve a better understanding of God.

This morning Ruth & I spoke to the Sunday School Class thanking them for the Christmas presents they brought to be sent to children in the relocation camps. The group was about in the 10 year old group — extremly naive as far as I could see, but to hear them sing a few hymns really was inspiring. I sure hope our 10 year olds don't become cynical & hard because of the evacuation. There couldn't be a worse trag[e]dy.

This evening Dr. Carl Shultz of University of Penn. reported to the Friends' Society of the decisions & general principles of post-war reconstruction arrived at at a convention of Friends in Ohio. It really opened my eyes to the tremendous problems. Some of the principles advocated were breaking away of all tarriff barriers (internationl free trade), an international polictical organization implying an international police force, and some international medium of exchange. In the discussion period one important or should I say novel point was brought out — the fact that we are at the present time giving away goods in the lend-lease program. In the post war period then, why can't we keep on giving away goods since the demand will be just as great or & greater & just adjust the tax on income to fill the national debt incurred. This would mean that the present full-employment would continue anyway so the people should be able to pay more taxes. I'm kinda hazy on most some of the ideas myself, so this shouldn't make much sense to you, but I hope to gain some knowledge on the problem thru that econ. course I'm taking & thru other lectures and books.

In addition to the economics problems there are also the spiritual and social problems such as religion, and racial prejudice and the various nationalisms. I was talking with a man after the meeting and he said that he was hoping that there was (students out here) they were hoping to get contacts with people in the camps who might fit certain jobs as they located them. You wouldn't wanta move out here would you? The whole family. Seriously, though, I really believe that we're not going to be able to live in Japanese communities after the war; nor do I think it desireable nor possible. We're going to have to decide whether we want to live in America & be Americans & be really assimilated, or whether we want to go to Japan & be Japanese. The issei have had a hard time over here, & I can't really blame them if they should decide to go back if they think it is more desireable. The nisei haven't had it too easy either; however, I feel that we nisei have an obligation to give American democracy another chance, to overcome racial prejudice, to work for international understanding, in spite of the discrimination we have experienced heretofore. I admit that we they have a pretty good excuse, but our leaving America would be utter condemnation of democracy in the eyes of the nations of the world & we have to admit that even though America hasn't lived up to all of its principles, its a pretty good theory & better than some forms of government.

Well, these are some of the thoughts I've been thinking & it might be good for you to be thinking along the same line. Who knows, but that they'll be shipping us all off someplace anyway, but then — thinking is good for you & it'll never hurt you.

George.
