Dec. 6, 1942

Hi!

I haven't heard from you for quite a number of days. I take it that camp is the same as it ever was -- the same routine day after day. Well, in a way I hope that it is because I know that while you're in there - at least your daily necessities are provided -- that is physically anyway. But you can be sure that I pray each night that the war will be over so that we may really live usefully even if it is more dangerous or hard or otherwise.

This is the first anniversary of the event which lead to the changing of the whole pattern of our lives isn't it? Remember that Sunday night we unconcernedly continued with the welcome home party for Masao Watanabe? If I recall correctly we had turkey that night. Right? We couldn't believe our ears what we heard over the radio + remember how pop got sore (almost lost his temper) because mom couldn't understand what happened -- she kept claiming that she didn't understand what "attack" meant. Even on that first night we got the first warnings as to what we could expect -- remember how the reports that people we knew were being picked up by the F.B.I. began coming over the radio? Then those months - terrible months - of expectation - waiting - wondering when our turn came next - when we would come home from school + find pop gone, or find the F.B.I. searching our home. Then all those books + papers we burned; I guess I took the ash out of the stove almost every day + we were glad that it was winter so we could burn the stove. Fortunately we were spared most of the terrors - imagine how Mrs Ogama felt, when he was picked up. Then the first shadows of evacuation came over the horizon. Remember how curiously we looked at those pictures of Manzanar in "Life"? It didn't seem possible. Then it came nearer - Alameda, Oakland, SF., Suisun. Then Willie came home from Berkeley + those last few months after it became evident that we too were going to have our turn but when? We bought jeans + boots + dried vegetables + milled, but still, it didn't seem real.

Then the order came -- seemed like every damned telephone pole had the sign on. Then we got busy. The order came about Fri. May 15, maybe Thurs. + we left home on the next Fri. th 22nd. Boy we really packed didn't we? And the store -- we didn't get rid of it till Wed. Willie, remember how we sold? It makes me sick to think about it. $35 - $5, or say $4 + they'll say 50¢. I learned a lot about human nature then - once a fellow knows you're helpless, he'll exploit you to the limit - well, some people anyway. Well, we were glad to get 300 for the store. Then we were in Walerga. When we first got there it seemed like a picnic - all our friends there crowding around. Then the adjustments we had to make - living in a single room, common mess hall, latrines, etc. Well, pop sure made up for lost sleep too didn't he? Then Walerga fades out + we moved to Tule Lake. You're still there + I'm here at college. Its seems so unreal -- sounds so impossible, + yet, there's no denying that what has happened + is now history. God sure does queer things, but I put my faith in him + follow his bidding for surely he is our only hope, our only foundation + our shepherd.

In retrospect, in looking back over our evacuation, I see many things - people of different classes thrown together - the rich living the same as the poor, people idly sitting around, others making the most out of it; a socialistic society if there ever was one -- but with the people living there under involuntary circumstances. It might've been an interesting experiment in socialism had everyone there been there voluntarily + were interested in working for the common good, but under the present conditions, I have my doubts.

The evacuation + probably my relocation here in college has had one very important effect upon my life. I have begun to realize that there is a God. And it is my sincere hope that I may learn to understand him. How else is it possible that I could be here in such a wonderful college in such a time when I hadn't even hoped to attend U.C. for a year? Surely there is a God who watches us, and takes care of us. Before, I thought that I was leading an insignificant, inconsequential, self centered life, but surely, I must have been put here for some purpose. I pray that I might be well prepared for whatever tasks I face.

The war isn't over yet, and there are over a hundred thousand of us in Relocation camps. The history of the Japanese Americans is still in the making. I shudder to think what it will be like after the war. How will we face life mentally, that is, what will be our attitudes, will we have the skills + knowledge, will we give up easily; how about our finances, will we be fit physically - to put in a real days work, can we cooperate with others; many, many such questions rise up in my mind + remain unanswered. I'm afraid that our showing will depend on our education + experience before evacuation and that the longer we remain in camps, the less chance we have. I certainly hope I'm wrong.

Well, so ends my thoughts for today Dec. 7, 1942 (its 1:15 AM now). I'll tell you what I did over the weekend tomorrow. It didn't seem fitting to include it in this letter + besides its late and I've run out of paper. This is the last sheet in the tablet I brought from Tule Lake.

George.
