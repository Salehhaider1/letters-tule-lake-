Sun, Jan 10, 1943

Hi! Will, how's everything? Still haven't heard from Willie about school. I expect that I'll hear everything about it soon. If you haven't heard from the NSR Council, you ought to write them and ask what the score is, because Dean Hunt definitely told me that they had written him and said that you should be able to come, and there's no doubt about the college's willingness to have you here.

Oh yeah, last Friday night, I met Mr. Howard K. Beale, former West Coast director of the NSRC, at a concert quaint in Clothier. He said that he was out here on the East Coast trying to raise some more money. The concert was a program by Alexander Kipnis, Basso, of the Metropolitan Opera Company. His singing, as far as my ability to listen, was flawless, but I think I enjoy women soprano, etc, better as soloists, although I do like men's voices too - maybe it was because none of the songs he sang were familiar to me.

Last night I saw a basketball game between our Freshman Team and the Friends Central High School - we lost 25-24, but the game wasn't very exciting because the teams were both as lousy. Then I went to see a movie in Clothier. The movie feature was "Blockade," a picture of the civil war in Spain 1937 or so. It starts out with a picture of a peaceful valley, with people tending their flocks and tilling the soil. Then comes war with a scene of people evacuating the valley which has become a battlefield. One other scene which remains viewed in my mind is the bombing, the destruction and murder of cities and its inhabitants. Oh yeah, one other, the available population being starved by blockade. Why in the (xxx) such conditions have to exist in a society where we are capable of so high a standard of living that we should be fighting and killing and destroying?

In our economics class right now we are studying business cycles. One theory that our instructor pointed out was that wasteful use of money in wars, or building of pyramids, wasteful as they may be, is an effective way to increase the money income of a nation and thus the periodic depression could be prevented. At the present time such is the case; we're in a war, wasting resources, evacuating a part of the population and building enormous internment camps, what for? To increase the money income of the nation? The fallacy in that theory to my thinking is that the social costs are not taken into account. Who can say that it is with killing a million men so that the remaining population can have a little more money to judge in their pockets?

I took a few hours to read another book too. "The Freedom of Man" by Arthur H. Compton. The author is an electric phycicist. The book is an essay on the question of whether science can ever replace religion. Mr. Compon's argument is that science is compatible with religion. Some scientists agree that whatever man does is predetermined and purely mechanical just as the laws of nature. The author argues that within certain limits we do follow certain laws, but just as in the case of atomic physics, we are free to decide right from wrong, and to guide the course of life. Then he discussed the concept of God, immortality, etc - very interesting but hard to understand, but he rests it in a fairly reasonable line of thinking.

I rest a box of candy yesterday as birthday present to pop. Hope it reaches you in one piece. I was thinking of sending some saccharine because you might not have enough sugar to make the coffee sweet enough, but since everybody can enjoy candy, I decided on it. I thought of a carton of cajarther too, but figured you could get them in camp cheaper anyway. If you do want saccharine, just say the word and I'll get some.

Well, so much for now, hope you had a nice, peaceful, and restful Sunday. May we continue to receive the blessings of our Father which is in heaven.

So long,

George.
